    Revisiting this assignment showed me that my only mistakes — questions 11 and 12 — came from a slip in the percent arithmetic, not a misunderstanding of the concept. Once I carefully substituted into score.two = 1.1(80) + 9.5 = 97.5, the prediction was straightforward, and the error of prediction followed directly as 100 − 97.5 = 2.5. It was a good reminder that one early arithmetic error (the prediction) can cascade into a second wrong answer (the error).

    Activity 1: Creating a Boxplot

    An in-class assignment shows how to create and interpret a boxplot, analyze its distribution and skewness, and determine the most appropriate measure of central tendency.

    Why I chose it

    I selected this assignment because it taught me to summarize and present data responsibly, skills directly applicable to report writing. Understanding when to use a boxplot instead of a bar chart, how to identify skewness, and when the median provides a more accurate representation than the mean are precisely the types of decisions required when presenting data to an audience that must trust your conclusions.

    Compare the original to the revision

    1. Create a boxplot of these data using this app.
    2. Is the resulting boxplot accurate? How can you tell?

      Yes. The accuracy of the boxplot can be verified by calculating the five-number summary and comparing it to the plot. When sorted, the data are 70, 78, 78, 79, 82, 87, 91, 93, 95, and 96, resulting in a minimum of 70, a first quartile (Q1) of 78, a median of 84.5, a third quartile (Q3) of 93, and a maximum of 96. These values correspond precisely to the box edges (78 and 93), the median line (84.5), and the whisker ends (70 and 96), confirming the accuracy of the boxplot.

    3. Label the 1st quartile, the 2nd quartile/median, and 3rd quartile.

      The first quartile (Q1) is 78, representing the left edge of the box. The second quartile (median) is 84.5, indicated by the line inside the box. The third quartile (Q3) is 93, corresponding to the right edge of the box. Therefore, the interquartile range (IQR), which encompasses the middle 50% of players, spans 15 seconds.

    4. Why is a boxplot better than a bar plot for this data set?

      A boxplot provides a comprehensive summary of the distribution of a single quantitative variable, including its center, spread, range, and skew, within a single visual representation. In contrast, a bar plot displays one bar per player and presents only individual values, thereby obscuring the median, quartiles, and the overall distributional shape. Because the objective is to understand the distribution of agility across the group, the boxplot effectively conveys this summary, whereas a bar plot does not.

    5. Is this data set skewed? If so, is the tail to the left or right?

      The data exhibit near symmetry with a slight left (negative) skew. In the boxplot, the left whisker (70 to 78, a span of 8) is noticeably longer than the right whisker (93 to 96, a span of 3), indicating that the distribution's tail extends to the left. This mild skewness is supported by the similarity between the mean (84.9) and median (84.5).

    6. Within this group, what is the best way to determine the central tendency of agility? Why?

      The median (84.5) represents the most appropriate single measure in this context. The boxplot indicates a slightly longer left tail, making the median preferable due to its resistance to skewness and outliers, while the mean is influenced by the extended tail. In this dataset, the mean (84.9) and median (84.5) are nearly identical, indicating general agreement; however, the median remains the more robust and representative statistic.

    Activity 2: Catching a Fraudulent Study

    This in-class activity focuses on the chi-square ($\chi^2$) test, demonstrating how to compare observed counts with expected values from a fair, random process and how to identify results that are unlikely to be due to chance.

    Why I chose it

    I missed class on the day hypothesis testing was discussed, so I selected this activity to ensure that I genuinely understood the $\chi^2$ test rather than simply recognizing its name. The fraud-detection context was particularly appealing to me because it demonstrates the $\chi^2$ test being applied in practical scenarios, such as determining whether a published “random sample” is plausible or if the data indicate possible tampering.

    The scenario

    Suppose you’ve come across a medical study that you think is fraudulent. The authors said they conducted a random control trial on a new medication, but you found that 120 of the 200 people in the trial were born in an even year. Test whether this is truly a random sample.

    Setting up the test

    The $\chi^2$ test compares observed counts to expected counts under the null hypothesis. The test statistic is:

    $$\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}$$
    • $H_0$: the sample is random — even-year and odd-year births occur in the expected 50/50 proportion.
    • $H_a$: the sample is not random — the proportions differ from 50/50.
    • $\alpha = 0.05$

    The math

    Group Observed (O) Expected (E) $\dfrac{(O - E)^2}{E}$
    Born in even year 120 100 $\dfrac{(120-100)^2}{100} = 4$
    Born in odd year 80 100 $\dfrac{(80-100)^2}{100} = 4$
    Total 200 200 $\chi^2 = 8$

    With one degree of freedom ($df = 1$, two categories minus one), $\chi^2 = 8$ gives a p-value of about $p \approx 0.0047$. Using the Excel function =CHISQ.TEST(observed_range, expected_range).

    Conclusion

    The p-value of approximately $0.0047$ is well below the significance threshold of $\alpha = 0.05$ (and also below $0.01$), leading to the rejection of the null hypothesis. A 120/80 split deviates substantially from the expected 100/100 distribution under random sampling, making it unlikely to have occurred by chance alone. While this result does not constitute proof of data fabrication, it provides a reviewer with strong statistical justification to question how a purportedly random sample could produce such a non-random outcome.

    Summary of what I learned

    Working through this activity outside of class clarified two key points for me. First, “expected” in the context of the $\chi^2$ test does not refer to what one hopes to observe; rather, it denotes the outcome predicted by the null hypothesis if no unusual effects are present. Second, the test operates in an intuitive manner: it aggregates the extent to which each category deviates from its expected value, with each deviation weighted according to the magnitude of the expected count. Thus, a small deviation from a large expected count is less significant than the same deviation from a smaller expected count. Understanding the formula in this way made the application to fraud detection seem logical, as the $\chi^2$ test effectively functions as a quantitative measure of suspicion.

    The Great British Bake Off By the Numbers

    Introduction

    In this statistical report, we will analyze a dataset from The Great British Bake Off (GBBO). In addition to providing an overview of the observations and variables, it will highlight the winners, the content's age, gender, and race, as well any missing data. The central questions explored in this report concern who competes and who wins, particularly with respect to age, gender expression, and apparent race. By examining distributions and frequencies across these variables, this report aims to illuminate patterns that may reflect broader social dynamics within the show. The dataset includes 192 contestants across 16 series, with variables ranging from demographic information to competition performance.

    The Data Set

    As stated in the introduction, this data set is derived from the GBBO, but let’s dive deeper into the variable types and what we can glean from them. The nominal variables in the data are Gender Expression, Apparent Race, Occupation, Hometown, Series, and Publisher. There are only a handful of interval and ratio variables: Age, Number of Star Bakers, and Ranking. Lastly, we have only one ordinal variable in the data set: Placement. With that in mind, the following charts and visuals will show when different statistical measures are appropriate, how to read distributions for both numerical and categorical data, and why it is important to let the data guide our analysis rather than relying on assumptions.

    Who Are Our Winners

    Referring to Figure 1, we can see the winners of each series, their gender expression, age, occupation, the number of times they won Star Baker, and their approximate hometown. So, let’s break it down: out of all the bakers, nine out of 16 are women, meaning seven out of 16 are men, or 56.25% & 43.75%, respectively. As for the winners' ages, we have a minimum of 20, a mean of 32.50, a median of 31, a mode of 30, and a maximum of 60 (an age range of 40). Moving on to Star Baker, we have a minimum of 0, a mean of 1.88, a median of 2, a mode of 2, and a maximum of 5. David Atherton of Series ten won with zero Star Bakers, the only contestant to do so (Edd from Series one is marked as zero Star Baker because the title was not given out during that series). Lastly, the winners’ locations are scattered around central England, with one each in Wales and Scotland.

    Gender and Race

    Breaking down Figure 2 of the contestants and winners, we can see that 9 winners were presented as women, and 97 contestants were presented as women; 13 winners were presented as white, whereas 140 contestants were presented as white. Drilling down into this, we can see that seven of the 16 winners are white women, and 68 of the contestants were white women. This yields 43.75% and 35.42%, respectively, an 8.33-percentage-point gap, indicating that white women win the show at a higher-than-expected rate. It’s worth noting that the number of white contestants is declining slightly in later series.

    There are several better ways to account for racial identities assigned to a single race. First, and the best in this case, is to add Multiracial or Mixed categories, since this data is based on observed rather than reported data. Adding one of these categories allows for the acceptance of multiracial identities without assigning them to the contestant. Beyond adding the Multiracial or Mixed categories, the data set could allow multiple racial identities for each contestant. The best option would be to use self-identification, but because not all contestants do, this is not possible with this data.

    How Old?

    Examining Figure 3, we can see the range of the contestants is 17 – 71 or 54 years; we have a mean of 37.57, a median of 33, and a mode of 31. Looking at our histogram, we can see the distribution is right-skewed, with most contestants between 30-39 years of age, followed by those between 20-29 years of age.

In addition to expanding this data, a complementary dataset on the judges could help identify any bias they may have.

    This descriptive analysis of The Great British Bake Off identifies several notable patterns. Winners are generally younger than the overall contestant pool, with a mean winning age of 32.50 compared to 37.57 for all contestants. White women are overrepresented among winners relative to their proportion in the contestant pool, winning at a rate 8.33 percentage points higher than expected. The age distribution of contestants is right-skewed, with a peak in the 30–39 range. These findings prompt further inquiry into which forms of culinary creativity are recognized and rewarded.     Summary of what I learned

    The most significant technique I encountered during this project was the application of the complement principle. This principle addresses questions such as, "What is the probability of seeing at least one of my fours in my opening hand?" Although this initially appears to require summing over four possible outcomes, reframing the question as "What is the probability of seeing none?" reduces the problem to a single P(X = 0) calculation. Subtracting this value from one provides the desired probability.

    This same approach underlies the inclusion-exclusion formula presented at the bottom of the infographic. For example, P(at least one land and at least one castable spell) is more efficiently computed by subtracting the probability of neither event occurring from one, rather than by enumerating all joint outcomes directly. I had previously encountered the complement principle in class, but observing each "at least one" scenario in the widget reduced to a single P(X = 0) calculation demonstrated its value as a practical analytical tool rather than merely a theoretical concept.
