    Homework 1: Data, Bias, and Study Design

    A quiz on the core concepts of research methods, including data collection, sampling bias, and experimental design.

    Why I chose it

    I selected this assignment because it addresses the essential principles one must adhere to when writing a research report, including proper data collection and formatting, identifying bias in sources, and designing studies that support valid conclusions. This assignment serves as a concise reference that can be consulted whenever a reminder of the criteria for valid research is needed.

    Summary of what I learned

    Revisiting this assignment enabled me to progress from rote memorization of formulas to a deeper comprehension of their underlying logic. Re-examining the marble, dice, and coin problems reinforced the addition and multiplication rules. The binomial question proved most valuable; I discovered that my error stemmed from failing to reframe the problem in binary terms (drawing it out).

    Activity 1: Creating a Boxplot

    An in-class assignment showing how to create and interpret a boxplot and how to analyze its distribution, skewness, and the most appropriate measure of central tendency.

    Why I chose it

    I selected this assignment because it taught me to summarize and present data responsibly, skills directly applicable to report writing. Understanding when to use a boxplot instead of a bar chart, how to identify skewness, and when the median provides a more accurate representation than the mean are precisely the types of decisions required when presenting data to an audience that must trust your conclusions.

    Summary of what I learned

    Revisiting this assignment made me recognize the impact that small design choices have on the learning experience. By removing the nationality column and pre-sorting the data, I was compelled to evaluate which information was essential for the task and which elements constituted unnecessary distractions. This process of distilling the assignment to its core components deepened my understanding of the underlying concepts.

    A descriptive analysis of The Great British Bake Off — who competes, who wins, and how age, gender expression, and apparent race are distributed across 16 series. Use the toggle to compare the original report with the revised version.

    Introduction

    In this statistical report, we will analyze a dataset from The Great British Bake Off (GBBO). In addition to providing an overview of the observations and variables, it will highlight the winners, the contestants’ age, gender, and race, and any missing data. The central questions explored concern who competes and who wins, particularly with respect to age, gender expression, and apparent race. The dataset includes 192 contestants across 16 series.

    Who Are Our Winners

    Out of all the bakers, nine of 16 are women and seven of 16 are men — 56.25% and 43.75% respectively. The winners’ ages have a minimum of 20, a mean of 32.50, a median of 31, a mode of 30, and a maximum of 60. For Star Baker: a minimum of 0, a mean of 1.88, a median of 2, a mode of 2, and a maximum of 5. David Atherton of Series ten won with zero Star Bakers, the only contestant to do so.

    Gender and Race

    Seven of the 16 winners are white women, and 68 of the contestants were white women — 43.75% and 35.42% respectively, an 8.33-percentage-point gap, indicating white women win at a higher-than-expected rate.

    How Old?

    The range is 17–71 (54 years); mean 37.57, median 33, mode 31. The distribution is right-skewed, with most contestants between 30–39, followed by 20–29.

    Conclusion

    Winners are generally younger than the overall pool (mean winning age 32.50 vs 37.57). White women are overrepresented among winners. The age distribution is right-skewed. Key limitations: observer-assigned race and gender, no culinary-training or education data, and no episode-level scoring.
